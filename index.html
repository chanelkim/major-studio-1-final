The Index of American Design

The Index of American Design (1936-1942) — a Federal Art Project from FDR's New Deal administration, an effort to provide work relief to artists during the Great Depression — produced an image collection of over 18,000 works on paper, now held by the National Gallery of Art. The project's documentation of American material culture not only serves as an invaluable visual archive but also reveals networks-of-influence that shaped an aesthetic legacy.
A broad quantitative analysis begins with a simple count of contributions per name, serving as a visual overview of the most prolific renderers in the collection. The largely anonymous “American 20th Century” attribution begs the question of what we might glean from the remaining named artists. Could individuals be linked by subject matter? Curatorial choices underline this effort toward a “usable past”, which hint at intentional groupings. With qualitative text analysis, this project hopes to reveal the connections between the artists whose trace we can follow and lend contextual homes for the ones left anonymous.
Thematic categories were borrowed from the physical microfiche catalog. These themes, categories, subcategories, and subtypes built a word dictionary, through a Natural Language Process called stemming.

The Partnership

While the Index offers a vast pictorial archive, intended to leave a “usable” American aesthetic, the metadata provides an alternate perspective to begin studying this historic federal project.
Textual analysis is the backbone of this proposal. By following through lines between units — the artists, the artworks, the administrators — beginning with the titles, we visualize shared connections and speculate on tensions affected by scale, intention, and social relationships.

The Designer

Chanel Kim is a designer and researcher based in New York City. She is currently pursuing a Master of Science in Data Visualization at Parsons School of Design. As a full-time digital product designer, she holds a Bachelor of Science in Human and Biological Anthropology, and work experience spanning creative campaigns, fine arts and small business adminstration, grassroots organizing, and hospitality. Material culture and the notion of leisure is her subject matter focus as she studies the intersection of data, design, technology, and social justice.
